Higher Education: The New Global Economic War
ENGLAND

By: Jane Doe
CAI3 - Group 3

In England, access to higher education has become closely tied to money and debt. Over the past decades, universities have shifted from being mostly publicly funded to operating in a market-driven system. Today, students are expected to finance their education through tuition fees and loans, which means that many begin their professional lives already owing thousands of pounds.

Currently, tuition fees for “home” students in England are capped at around £9,250 per year, while international students can pay anywhere between £12,000 and £38,000 or more annually, depending on the university and program. Prestigious institutions such as University of Oxford, University of Cambridge, or London School of Economics often charge some of the highest fees, reinforcing the idea that top-quality education comes at a very high cost.

This creates a system where access is technically open, but not equally accessible. While more students can enter university than before, not everyone can afford the same opportunities. Students from wealthier backgrounds face fewer financial risks, while others must carefully consider whether the long-term debt is worth it.

In contrast, a public university in Colombia, such as the Universidad Pedagogica Nacional, offers significantly lower tuition fees, often adjusted according to a student’s economic situation. This makes higher education more accessible to a wider population, even though resources and global prestige may differ from elite English institutions.

Overall, the situation in England reflects a broader transformation of higher education into an economic investment. The system expands access in numbers, but at the same time raises an important question: is education truly a right, or has it become a privilege that depends on the ability to pay?
