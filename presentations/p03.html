Higher Education: The New Global Economic War
CHINA

By: David Delgado
CAI3 - Group 3

Hello everyone, this presentation explains how expensive and difficult it is to access higher education in China, and what this means for students. In China, access to university is highly competitive due to the Gaokao, a national exam that determines students’ futures. For many years, it was seen as a way to achieve social mobility, especially for low-income students. For example, studying at Peking University, one of the most prestigious universities in China, costs between 14,000 and 35,000 yuan per semester for local students, and up to 56,000 yuan for international students. This shows that even if education is more affordable than in some countries, it still requires a significant financial effort.

In terms of access and percentages, millions of students take the Gaokao every year, but less than 2% are admitted to elite universities, while a much larger percentage enters lower-tier institutions or does not enter university at all. At the same time, China produces more than 12 million graduates each year, but not all of them find jobs. There are several circumstances that affect access and outcomes. First, there is an oversupply of graduates. Second, there is a mismatch between university education and labor market needs. Third, the economic slowdown limits job opportunities.

Regarding laws and policies, the government has implemented reforms such as the Vocational Education Law of China, which promotes technical and vocational education. It also supports employment programs, internships, and entrepreneurship. In terms of comparison, education in China is generally cheaper than in the United States, but much more competitive. In contrast, some European systems offer more balance between academic and vocational education. Another important factor is migration. Many graduates move to large cities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen in search of opportunities, but they face high living costs and intense competition.

In conclusion, although entering university in China requires great effort, a degree no longer guarantees employment, which creates uncertainty for many young people. Thank you.
