Higher Education: The New Global Economic War
USA

By: Daniel Echeverry
CAI3 - Group 3

Which country has the most expensive and famous universities in the world? The United States does. For example, studying a degree at the University of Chicago can cost about $70,000 per semester for studying. This is a lot of money. Another important point is the admission rate. At this universities, the admission rate is very low, between 4% and 6%. This means that out of 100 students who apply, only 5 or 6 get in. Universities in the United States are decentralized. The government helps students with scholarships and financial help. However, studying there is still very expensive. Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), or Stanford University, and Harvard University have tuition costs between $60,000 and $90,000 dollars per semester.

Today, education is also a business, like investing in the stock market, and universities in the United States know this very well. These universities look for students with strong critical thinking and math skills. But they also look for students who have enough money to pay for their studies. So, what is the purpose of ranking the most famous and expensive universities in the world? Is it just a competition of egos?

A contrast with UPN University

Universidad Pedagógica Nacional is a public university in Colombia that focuses on teacher education. Unlike many private universities, the tuition is very low, around 50,000 Colombian pesos per semester. This makes it accessible for many students. The university is well known for its strong pedagogical approach and its long experience in the field of education. It prepares future teachers with a solid knowledge and practical skills for the classroom.

However, entering this university is not easy. The admission process is very strict and has three main filters. First, students must have a good score on the Saber 11 exam. Then, they must pass a specific admission test. Finally, they have an interview to evaluate their motivation and abilities. These steps ensure that only highly committed students are accepted. But if education is a right, why are the admission processes so selective even in a public university?
